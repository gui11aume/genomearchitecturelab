E. coli electroporation

This protocol has been optimized for efficiency. A major source of failure during bacteria electroporation is the occurrence of an electric arc, which is usually due to the presence of salts in the solution. Electric arcs sometimes produce a visible and audible flash, but they can also go unnoticed. Some machines indicate the presence of an arc on the display, if it occurs. Since DNA is usually kept in buffers containing salts, it is important to use a small volume of solution. It is also important to respect the volumes indicated in the protocol, as they determine the resistance of the solution and the efficiency of the shock. By pressing the “actual time” button, you can see the time constant of the shock. The value should be between 4.0 and 5.0. If not, an electric arc probably occurred. It is recommended that you write down the time constant of every electroporation for troubleshooting purposes.

Material:

  • 50 μL competent cells
  • Bio-Rad 0.1 cm cuvette (REF: 165-2089)
  • Bio-Rad Gene Pulser II (REF: 165-2105)
  • plasmid DNA
  • LB plates with proper antibiotics

Procedure:

  1. Take competent cells from -80°C freezer and thaw them on ice.
  2. Put the cuvette on ice.
  3. Pipette 1-2 μL of plasmid DNA into 50 μL competent cells when thawed. These volumes have a major impact on the electric shock, increasing them may cause an electric arc.
  4. Transfer the mix into the cuvette.
  5. Choose programme Ec1 or set voltage to 2.5 kV on Gene Pulser II.
  6. Place the cuvette in the holder, press the “shock” button(s).
  7. Remove the cuvette from holder, immediately add 150 μL LB to the cuvette.
  8. Spread everything on LB plate
  9. Keep LB plates at 37°C and pick colonies after more than 12 hours incubation.
